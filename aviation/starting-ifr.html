Starting IFR Training

Flying Blind

I’m starting this blog to journal my IFR training, and as a way of keeping track of my progress in my aviation journey.

My strategy for IFR is to first complete all ground school segments related to the practical aspects of IFR flight. As of today (April 13th, 2024), I have mostly completed the Sporty’s IFR Ground School segments related to En Route Navigation, IFR Departures, and IFR Approaches. I plan to fully complete this portion of ground school this month, then progress to Weather, FAR Regulations, and then build upon that knowledge to prepare myself for the IFR written examination.

One thing that I decided when starting my IFR journey is that I would like to learn how to fly without GPS before transitioning to a Slant Golf or Technically Advanced aircraft. I’m unsure if this is the most cost-effective strategy, but I hope that it will force me to learn the fundamentals without using modern technology as a crutch. Ideally, this should build stronger situational and spatial awareness in the sky, even after I transition to fly GPS after I receive my instrument rating.

First Sim Flight

For my first sim flight, I flew out of Paso Robles (KPRB) in non-precision approach conditions in my instructor’s home X-Plane simulator. I practiced the following skills in the lesson:

  • flying the KPRB ODP for Runway 13
  • intercepting VOR radials
  • modifying course to comply with Radar Vectors
  • intercepting a DME Arc (0.5 nm before, using the 0.5 * 10% of ground track rule)
  • tracking a DME Arc
    • using the 10-10 rule, where I add 10 to the OBS when turning right, then turn right 10 degrees until I intercept the next radial. This rule is inverted for left turns.
    • using the G1000’s bearing pointer, where I aligned the bearing pointer with the right/left side of the HSI every 10 degrees.
  • diving and driving using the vertical navigation panel of the approach plate. For this technique, I descended at about ~1900 RPM / 600 fpm.
  • executing a missed approach at MDA

I also practiced flying Manhattan Regional’s (KMHK) DME Arc Approach, where I practiced the following additional skills:

  • briefing an approach plate
  • briefing an ODP
  • operating with a VOR for DME, a secondary VOR for radial interception, and a localizer
  • intercepting a glideslope for an ILS approach
  • executing a missed approach at DA
